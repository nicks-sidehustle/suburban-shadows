Tonight, we're in Columbia, Illinois — a peaceful community of 10,000 residents. Here, one family seemed to embody everything suburban America aspired to be. But in May 2009, this community learned a horrifying truth: sometimes the greatest danger comes from the very person sworn to keep others safe.

Chris Coleman was living the American Dream. At 32, he was head of security for Joyce Meyer Ministries, protecting one of America's most prominent televangelists. His $100,000 salary supported a picture-perfect life in a cream-colored colonial on Robert Drive. He was married to his high school sweetheart Sheri, and they had two sons — Garett, 11, and Gavin, 9.

To his neighbors, Chris was the friendly guy who coached Little League and helped elderly Mrs. Patterson with her groceries. The family attended Grace Church every Sunday, sitting in the same pew. Coleman's job took him around the world, coordinating security for international speaking engagements. His colleagues respected his expertise and professionalism.

But in October 2008, the Colemans began receiving disturbing threats. Anonymous emails that knew intimate details about their daily routine. "I know where your boys go to school," one message read. "I know when Sheri goes to the grocery store alone."

As a security professional, Coleman handled the threats perfectly. He contacted police, upgraded his home security system, and varied the family's routines. He documented every threat with meticulous precision. The messages escalated — graffiti on their garage, a dead bird left on their doorstep.

The psychological impact was devastating. Sheri became increasingly anxious and withdrawn. The boys couldn't understand why they couldn't ride their bikes anymore, why Mommy seemed so scared all the time.

What nobody knew was that Chris Coleman was living a double life. While maintaining his image as a devoted Christian family man, Coleman was conducting an intense affair with Tara Lintz, a woman from Florida. Their relationship had been going on for years — passionate emails, secret phone calls, promises of a future together.

But Coleman faced a problem. Joyce Meyer Ministries had strict moral clauses. Divorce wasn't just frowned upon — it could cost him his six-figure job. In his twisted calculation, Coleman saw only one solution. His family had to die.

Coleman's emails to Tara became increasingly desperate. "Sometimes I feel like there's no way out," he wrote in March 2009. "Everything I've worked for could all disappear. But I love you more than my own life. We'll find a way."

On May 5, 2009, Coleman put his plan into action. His security cameras captured him leaving home at 5:43 AM for what was supposed to be a business trip to Texas. But Coleman wasn't heading to the airport. He drove to a location where he'd hidden spare clothes and red spray paint, then returned home through a route designed to avoid cameras.

Using his intimate knowledge of his own security system, Coleman bypassed the alarms and entered through the basement. He moved through rooms where his children slept peacefully, past family photos and Little League trophies.

The murders were carried out with methodical precision. Sheri was found in the master bedroom, strangled while she slept. Eleven-year-old Garett was found in his bed, still wearing his Chicago White Sox t-shirt. Nine-year-old Gavin was discovered in his Superman pajamas, clutching his stuffed animal.

After killing his family, Coleman spray-painted threatening messages on the walls: "F--- YOU" and "PUNISHED." Then he left the same way he'd entered and continued his alibi charade.

At 6:43 AM, Coleman called his neighbor Ron Kowalski, claiming he was worried because Sheri wasn't answering her phone. When Kowalski entered the house with a spare key, he found the horrific crime scene and immediately called 911.

The small Columbia police department had never handled a triple homicide. FBI agents who'd been monitoring the threats against the Coleman family quickly joined the investigation. But investigators immediately found Coleman's story suspicious.

The complete absence of forced entry was troubling. Despite the sophisticated security system, there was no indication anyone had broken in. All windows were locked from inside, all doors showed no tampering, and the security system had never been triggered.

Cell phone records provided the first breakthrough. While Coleman claimed to be 60 miles away heading to Texas, his phone had pinged cell towers near his home between 5:43 and 6:30 AM. The technology doesn't lie.

Email analysis was devastating. The threatening messages supposedly sent by an anonymous stalker were traced back to computers Coleman had access to. Digital forensics found many threats were composed during times when Coleman was alone with his computer.

Most damaging was the affair evidence. Phone records showed hundreds of calls between Coleman and Tara Lintz in the weeks before the murders. Text messages revealed Coleman's desperation to escape his marriage. "I can't wait much longer," Coleman texted Lintz just two days before the murders. "Something has to change soon."

As evidence mounted, Coleman's facade began crumbling. His behavior at the funeral raised eyebrows — he seemed more concerned with his appearance than mourning his family. Phone records showed he'd been texting Tara Lintz while police processed the crime scene where his children lay dead.

Coleman also carried a $1 million life insurance policy on his wife and substantial policies on both children. If they'd been murdered by an external threat, he would have become a wealthy widower.

On April 19, 2011, Chris Coleman was convicted on three counts of first-degree murder and sentenced to life in prison without parole. The prosecution painted him as a narcissistic killer who valued his image and income more than his family's lives.

"Chris Coleman didn't just kill his wife and children," the prosecutor told the jury. "He terrorized them for months with anonymous threats. He made them live in fear in their own home, then used that terror as cover for their murder."

The jury deliberated just over four hours before returning guilty verdicts. Several jurors said Coleman's complete lack of emotion during the trial convinced them of his guilt more than any evidence.

The house at 6 Robert Drive sits empty now. Chris Coleman remains in prison, still maintaining his innocence. For the Columbia community, the hardest part isn't just that Coleman committed these crimes — it's that they lived alongside such calculated evil and never recognized the danger.

The Coleman case reminds us that evil doesn't always look like we expect. Sometimes it wears a security badge and a friendly smile. Sometimes it coaches Little League and volunteers at church. Sometimes it lives next door, behind a white picket fence.
